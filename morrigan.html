Irish Mythology: The Mórrígan

Within Irish mythology you can enter and access the otherworld through different points in the landscape, mist is seen as a veil to slip into another dimension.

The Mórrígan is a collection of supernatural women - a shapeshifting goddess of war, destiny, and fertility. Deeply tied to both warfare and the land, she serves a protective and tutelary role, embodying the complexities of life and death.

In our local reality, this reality right now that we can touch, we see the Morrigan as a crow. Crows are carrion birds, meaning they feast on already dead things. They were known to show up to battlefields and peck at the fallen once the battle had finished. The Morrigan’s association with the crow encapsulates her way of operating: she is a shadow that lingers on the battlefield, striking fear and confusion among soldiers to sway the tide of battle to her favoured side. The Morrigan’s power is not just in physical warfare but in also psychological dominance.

The Morrigan’s complex relationship with the warrior Cú Chulainn is a key tale in Irish mythology. Their story begins during the Táin Bó Cúailnge, when Cú Chulainn defended Ulster against an army led by Queen Medb. Between battles, the Morrigan attempted to seduce him, offering both her love and her aid in the fight.

Despite her beauty and power, Cú Chulainn rejected her advances. The Morrigan set out for revenge.

To hinder Cú Chulainn, she took the forms of a heifer, an eel, and a female wolf, challenging him throughout his battle with Queen Medb. In the end, Cú Chulainn was mortally wounded despite successfully holding off Medb's forces. Knowing his time had come, he tied himself to a large stone to die standing, facing his enemies. For three days, he remained upright, striking fear into those who dared approach.

Only when a crow perched on his shoulder, did his enemies know he had passed.

War and death gods are often seen as evil, but the Morrigan transcends such binaries. The Christian notion of dark and light as opposing forces does not apply here; the Morrigan is a complex and dominant force who embodies life and death as interdependent forces. The crow, being a carrion bird, thrives on death but ensures nothing is wasted, returning energy to the earth. Similarly, the Morrígan’s actions, though seemingly destructive, are part of a larger, natural order that sustains life.

The three folklore's you are engaging with revolve around the forest as a central setting. The forest itself is symbolic, acting as a liminal space between the known and the unknown, it is the theatre of superstition.

This interface sheds light onto the folklore that depict women as figures of power and agency. In some contexts, these narratives predate modern feminist movements, highlighting matriarchal societies where women assert dominance and defy traditional gender roles.

In these stories, the women are allowed to be complex and the typical societal power dynamics are reversed, with men becoming the subjects of manipulation and control, challenging the conventional notion of male authority.
